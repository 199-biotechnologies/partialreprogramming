# Comprehensive Partial Reprogramming & Cellular Rejuvenation Paper List

Last updated: 2026-04-03

---

## 1. FOUNDATIONAL

**1.1** "The Developmental Capacity of Nuclei taken from Intestinal Epithelium Cells of Feeding Tadpoles"
- **Authors:** Gurdon, J.B.
- **Journal:** Journal of Embryology and Experimental Morphology, 10, 622-640
- **Year:** 1962
- **DOI:** [10.1242/dev.10.4.622](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.10.4.622)
- **Summary:** Demonstrated that differentiated intestinal epithelial cell nuclei from feeding tadpoles could be transplanted into enucleated eggs and direct development of normal tadpoles, proving that somatic cell nuclei retain full genetic information for development.
- **Organism/Model:** *Xenopus laevis* (African clawed frog)

**1.2** "Induction of Pluripotent Stem Cells from Mouse Embryonic and Adult Fibroblast Cultures by Defined Factors"
- **Authors:** Takahashi, K. & Yamanaka, S.
- **Journal:** Cell, 126(4), 663-676
- **Year:** 2006
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2006.07.024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2006.07.024)
- **Summary:** Identified four transcription factors (Oct3/4, Sox2, c-Myc, Klf4) that can reprogram mouse fibroblasts into induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), establishing the foundation for all cellular reprogramming research. Nobel Prize-winning work.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse embryonic and adult fibroblasts

**1.3** "Induction of Pluripotent Stem Cells from Adult Human Fibroblasts by Defined Factors"
- **Authors:** Takahashi, K., Tanabe, K., Ohnuki, M. et al. (Yamanaka lab)
- **Journal:** Cell, 131(5), 861-872
- **Year:** 2007
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2007.11.019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2007.11.019)
- **Summary:** Extended the iPSC technology to human cells, demonstrating that the same four Yamanaka factors can reprogram adult human dermal fibroblasts into pluripotent stem cells resembling human embryonic stem cells.
- **Organism/Model:** Human adult dermal fibroblasts

## 2. THEORETICAL

**2.1** "The Strategy of the Genes"
- **Authors:** Waddington, C.H.
- **Publisher:** George Allen & Unwin, London
- **Year:** 1957
- **DOI:** N/A (book)
- **Summary:** Introduced the "epigenetic landscape" metaphor -- a ball rolling down a landscape of valleys representing cell fate decisions during development. This concept underpins modern understanding of cellular identity and reprogramming as movement through an epigenetic state space.
- **Organism/Model:** Theoretical/conceptual framework

**2.2** "The Hallmarks of Aging"
- **Authors:** Lopez-Otin, C., Blasco, M.A., Partridge, L., Serrano, M. & Kroemer, G.
- **Journal:** Cell, 153(6), 1194-1217
- **Year:** 2013
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2013.05.039](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2013.05.039)
- **Summary:** Defined nine hallmarks of aging (genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication) as a unifying framework for aging research.
- **Organism/Model:** Cross-species review (theoretical framework)

**2.3** "Hallmarks of Aging: An Expanding Universe"
- **Authors:** Lopez-Otin, C., Blasco, M.A., Partridge, L., Serrano, M. & Kroemer, G.
- **Journal:** Cell, 186(2), 243-278
- **Year:** 2023
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2022.11.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2022.11.001)
- **Summary:** Updated the original hallmarks framework to twelve hallmarks, adding disabled macroautophagy, chronic inflammation, and dysbiosis. Provides the most current organizing framework for understanding aging biology.
- **Organism/Model:** Cross-species review (theoretical framework)

**2.4** "Loss of Epigenetic Information as a Cause of Mammalian Aging"
- **Authors:** Yang, J.H., Hayano, M., Griffin, P.T. et al. (Sinclair lab)
- **Journal:** Cell, 186(2), 305-326.e27
- **Year:** 2023
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2022.12.027](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2022.12.027)
- **Summary:** Using an "ICE" (Inducible Changes to the Epigenome) mouse model, demonstrated that faithful DNA repair erodes the epigenetic landscape and drives aging phenotypes. Showed that OSK-mediated reprogramming can reverse these changes, supporting the Information Theory of Aging -- that aging is driven by loss of epigenetic information, not genetic mutations.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse (ICE model) and mouse cells

**2.5** "The Information Theory of Aging"
- **Authors:** Lu, Y.R., Tian, X. & Sinclair, D.A.
- **Journal:** Nature Aging, 3, 1486-1499
- **Year:** 2023
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s43587-023-00527-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s43587-023-00527-6)
- **Summary:** Formalizes the Information Theory of Aging (ITOA), proposing that aging is driven by progressive loss of youthful epigenetic information and that this information can be retrieved via epigenetic reprogramming to catalyze age reversal.
- **Organism/Model:** Theoretical review

**2.6** "Aging, Rejuvenation, and Epigenetic Reprogramming: Resetting the Aging Clock"
- **Authors:** Rando, T.A. & Chang, H.Y.
- **Journal:** Cell, 148(1-2), 46-57
- **Year:** 2012
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2012.01.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2012.01.003)
- **Summary:** Landmark review defining youthfulness and senescence as epigenetic states and proposing that the aging clock can be reversed by restoring characteristics of youthfulness to aged cells through epigenetic reprogramming.
- **Organism/Model:** Review (cross-species)

## 3. IN VIVO MOUSE -- PROGERIA MODELS

**3.1** "In Vivo Amelioration of Age-Associated Hallmarks by Partial Reprogramming"
- **Authors:** Ocampo, A., Reddy, P., Martinez-Redondo, P. et al. (Belmonte lab)
- **Journal:** Cell, 167(7), 1719-1733.e12
- **Year:** 2016
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2016.11.052](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2016.11.052)
- **Summary:** The foundational partial reprogramming paper. Showed that short-term cyclic expression of OSKM (2 days on, 5 days off) ameliorates cellular and physiological hallmarks of aging and extends lifespan by ~30% in progeroid (LAKI) mice, without causing teratomas. Established the cyclic induction paradigm.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- progeria model (Lmna G609G/G609G, LAKI)

**3.2** "A Single Short Reprogramming Early in Life Initiates and Propagates an Epigenetically Related Mechanism Improving Fitness and Promoting an Increased Healthy Lifespan"
- **Authors:** Alle, Q., Le Borgne, E., Bensadoun, P. et al.
- **Journal:** Aging Cell, 21(11), e13714
- **Year:** 2022
- **DOI:** [10.1111/acel.13714](https://doi.org/10.1111/acel.13714)
- **Summary:** Demonstrated that a single transient pulse of OSKM expression at 2 months of age is sufficient to prevent musculoskeletal deterioration, improve tissue structures in kidney, spleen, skin, and lung, and increase lifespan by ~15% when measured in old age -- implying a deep, persistent epigenetic memory effect of early-life reprogramming.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- wildtype and progeria models

## 4. IN VIVO MOUSE -- WILDTYPE

**4.1** "In Vivo Partial Reprogramming Alters Age-Associated Molecular Changes During Physiological Aging in Mice"
- **Authors:** Browder, K.C., Reddy, P., Yamamoto, M. et al. (Belmonte lab)
- **Journal:** Nature Aging, 2, 243-253
- **Year:** 2022
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s43587-022-00183-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s43587-022-00183-2)
- **Summary:** First study to demonstrate partial reprogramming benefits in naturally aging wildtype mice. Long-term cyclic OSKM expression led to rejuvenating effects in kidney and skin, associated with reversion of the epigenetic clock, reduced inflammation, senescence, and stress response gene expression.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- wildtype C57BL/6J

**4.2** "Multi-omic Rejuvenation of Naturally Aged Tissues by a Single Cycle of Transient Reprogramming"
- **Authors:** Chondronasiou, D., Gill, D., Vaquero, A. et al. (Serrano lab, CNIO)
- **Journal:** Aging Cell, 21(3), e13578
- **Year:** 2022
- **DOI:** [10.1111/acel.13578](https://doi.org/10.1111/acel.13578)
- **Summary:** Showed that a single 1-week period of transient OSKM expression is sufficient to reverse DNA methylation changes in the pancreas, liver, spleen, and blood of naturally aged (55-week-old) wildtype mice, with reversion of transcriptional changes in aging-related biological processes.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- wildtype (55-week-old)

**4.3** "Gene Therapy-Mediated Partial Reprogramming Extends Lifespan and Reverses Age-Related Changes in Aged Mice"
- **Authors:** Macip, C.C., Czajka, A., Selber-Hnatiw, S. et al. (Rejuvenate Bio)
- **Journal:** Cellular Reprogramming, 26(1), 24-32
- **Year:** 2024
- **DOI:** [10.1089/cell.2023.0072](https://doi.org/10.1089/cell.2023.0072)
- **Summary:** Systemically delivered AAV encoding inducible OSK to 124-week-old male mice, extending median remaining lifespan by 109% over wild-type controls while enhancing multiple health parameters. Demonstrated significant age-reversal in heart and liver as measured by DNA methylation clocks.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- wildtype C57BL/6J (extremely aged, 124 weeks)

**4.4** "Targeted Partial Reprogramming of Age-Associated Cell States Improves Markers of Health in Mouse Models of Aging"
- **Authors:** Macip, C.C., Hasan, R., Houghton, A. et al. (Turn Biotechnologies)
- **Journal:** Science Translational Medicine, 16(764), eadg1777
- **Year:** 2024
- **DOI:** [10.1126/scitranslmed.adg1777](https://doi.org/10.1126/scitranslmed.adg1777)
- **Summary:** Targeted OSK expression specifically to senescent (Cdkn2a-positive) cells using AAV delivery. Achieved a 12% increase in overall lifespan in wildtype male mice -- the largest lifespan extension by partial reprogramming in wildtype mice to date. Also reduced inflammation, restored tissue integrity, and enhanced wound healing in both progeroid and naturally aged mice.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- wildtype and progeroid models

## 5. TISSUE-SPECIFIC REPROGRAMMING

**5.1** "Reprogramming to Recover Youthful Epigenetic Information and Restore Vision"
- **Authors:** Lu, Y., Brommer, B., Tian, X. et al. (Sinclair lab)
- **Journal:** Nature, 588(7836), 124-129
- **Year:** 2020
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s41586-020-2975-4](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2975-4)
- **Summary:** Demonstrated that ectopic expression of OSK (without c-Myc) in mouse retinal ganglion cells restores youthful DNA methylation patterns, promotes axon regeneration after optic nerve injury, and reverses vision loss in glaucoma and aged mice. Required the DNA demethylases TET1 and TET2. No tumor formation observed over 10-18 months.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- retinal ganglion cells (eye)

**5.2** "Reversible Reprogramming of Cardiomyocytes to a Fetal State Drives Heart Regeneration in Mice"
- **Authors:** Chen, Y., Luttmann, F.F., Schoger, E. et al.
- **Journal:** Science, 373(6562), 1537-1540
- **Year:** 2021
- **DOI:** [10.1126/science.abg5159](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abg5159)
- **Summary:** Heart-specific transient OSKM expression induced adult cardiomyocytes to dedifferentiate to a fetal-like state, conferring regenerative capacity to adult hearts. Short-term expression before and during myocardial infarction ameliorated myocardial damage and improved cardiac function.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- cardiomyocytes (heart)

**5.3** "In Vivo Reprogramming Ameliorates Aging Features in Dentate Gyrus Cells and Improves Memory in Mice"
- **Authors:** Rodriguez-Matellan, A., Alcazar, N., Hernandez, F., Serrano, M. & Avila, J.
- **Journal:** Stem Cell Reports, 15(5), 1056-1066
- **Year:** 2020
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.stemcr.2020.09.010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stemcr.2020.09.010)
- **Summary:** Cyclic OSKM induction (3 days on, 4 days off) in 6-month-old wildtype mice over 4 months prevented age-dependent reduction of H3K9me3 in the dentate gyrus, elevated neurogenesis markers (doublecortin, calretinin), increased NMDA receptor subunit GluN2B levels, and improved object recognition memory.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- dentate gyrus (brain)

**5.4** "Expansion of the Neocortex and Protection from Neurodegeneration by In Vivo Transient Reprogramming"
- **Authors:** Shen, Y.R., Zaballa, S., Bech, X. et al. (Serrano, Klein, Giralt, del Toro labs)
- **Journal:** Cell Stem Cell, 31(12), 1741-1759.e8
- **Year:** 2024
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.stem.2024.09.013](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stem.2024.09.013)
- **Summary:** Controlled spatiotemporal induction of Yamanaka factors in the mouse brain led to progenitor expansion, increased upper cortical neurons and glia, and enhanced motor and social behavior. In the 5xFAD Alzheimer's model, controlled induction prevented development of several disease hallmarks.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- brain (neocortex), 5xFAD Alzheimer's model

## 6. HUMAN CELLS (IN VITRO)

**6.1** "Transient Non-Integrative Expression of Nuclear Reprogramming Factors Promotes Multifaceted Amelioration of Aging in Human Cells"
- **Authors:** Sarkar, T.J., Quarta, M., Mukherjee, S. et al. (Sebastiano lab, Stanford)
- **Journal:** Nature Communications, 11, 1545
- **Year:** 2020
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s41467-020-15174-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-15174-3)
- **Summary:** Used mRNA-mediated transient expression of OSKM+LIN28+NANOG in aged human endothelial cells, fibroblasts, and chondrocytes to reset the epigenetic clock, reduce inflammatory profiles, and restore youthful regenerative responses -- all without full dedifferentiation or loss of cell identity.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- endothelial cells, fibroblasts, chondrocytes (aged donors)

**6.2** "Multi-omic Rejuvenation of Human Cells by Maturation Phase Transient Reprogramming"
- **Authors:** Gill, D., Parry, A., Santos, F. et al. (Reik lab, Babraham Institute)
- **Journal:** eLife, 11, e71624
- **Year:** 2022
- **DOI:** [10.7554/eLife.71624](https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.71624)
- **Summary:** Developed "maturation phase transient reprogramming" (MPTR) -- expressing OSKM for a precise duration then withdrawing. Rejuvenated the transcriptome of human fibroblasts by ~30 years (measured by a novel transcriptome clock), restored youthful collagen production, and partially restored migration speed. Epigenome rejuvenated to similar extent.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- dermal fibroblasts (middle-aged donors)

**6.3** "Partial Reprogramming Induces a Steady Decline in Epigenetic Age Before Loss of Somatic Identity"
- **Authors:** Olova, N., Simpson, D.J., Marioni, R.E. & Chandra, T.
- **Journal:** Aging Cell, 18(1), e12877
- **Year:** 2019
- **DOI:** [10.1111/acel.12877](https://doi.org/10.1111/acel.12877)
- **Summary:** Mapped the kinetics of epigenetic rejuvenation vs. dedifferentiation during a 49-day iPSC reprogramming time-course. Identified a "safe window" (days 3-13) where epigenetic age steadily declines (~10 years by day 7, ~20+ years by day 11) before fibroblast identity is lost (day 15+), demonstrating that rejuvenation and dedifferentiation can be uncoupled.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- dermal fibroblasts

## 7. CHEMICAL REPROGRAMMING

**7.1** "Pluripotent Stem Cells Induced from Mouse Somatic Cells by Small-Molecule Compounds"
- **Authors:** Hou, P., Li, Y., Zhang, X. et al. (Deng lab, Peking University)
- **Journal:** Science, 341(6146), 651-654
- **Year:** 2013
- **DOI:** [10.1126/science.1239278](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1239278)
- **Summary:** First demonstration that pluripotent stem cells can be generated from mouse somatic cells using only seven small-molecule compounds (no transcription factors), achieving up to 0.2% efficiency. Established the feasibility of purely chemical reprogramming.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- somatic cells (fibroblasts)

**7.2** "Chemical Reprogramming of Human Somatic Cells to Pluripotent Stem Cells"
- **Authors:** Guan, J., Wang, G., Wang, J. et al. (Deng lab, Peking University)
- **Journal:** Nature, 605(7909), 325-331
- **Year:** 2022
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s41586-022-04593-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-04593-5)
- **Summary:** Extended chemical reprogramming to human cells for the first time, generating human chemically induced pluripotent stem cells (hCiPSCs) from human somatic cells using defined small-molecule cocktails without any genetic factors.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- somatic cells

**7.3** "Highly Efficient and Rapid Generation of Human Pluripotent Stem Cells by Chemical Reprogramming"
- **Authors:** Yang, Z., Wang, Y. et al. (Deng lab, Peking University)
- **Journal:** Cell Stem Cell, 30(5), 675-688.e10
- **Year:** 2023
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.stem.2023.02.008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.stem.2023.02.008)
- **Summary:** Dramatically shortened the chemical reprogramming timeline from ~50 days to a minimum of 16 days and achieved highly reproducible, efficient generation of hCiPSCs from all 17 tested donors, making chemical reprogramming clinically tractable.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- fibroblasts (17 donors)

**7.4** "Chemically Induced Reprogramming to Reverse Cellular Aging"
- **Authors:** Yang, J.H., Petty, C.A., Dixon-McDougall, T. et al. (Sinclair lab)
- **Journal:** Aging (Albany NY), 15(13), 5966-5989
- **Year:** 2023
- **DOI:** [10.18632/aging.204896](https://doi.org/10.18632/aging.204896)
- **Summary:** Identified six chemical cocktails that restore youthful genome-wide transcript profiles and reverse transcriptomic age in human cells within 4 days, without requiring genetic reprogramming factors. Provides a non-genetic chemical approach to age reversal.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- fibroblasts, other cell types

**7.5** "Chemical Reprogramming Ameliorates Cellular Hallmarks of Aging and Extends Lifespan"
- **Authors:** Multiple authors
- **Journal:** EMBO Molecular Medicine
- **Year:** 2025
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s44321-025-00265-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44321-025-00265-9)
- **Summary:** Showed that a seven-compound (7c) chemical cocktail reverses multiple aging hallmarks in human fibroblasts, and a reduced two-compound (2c) subset retains rejuvenating effects. In C. elegans, 2c treatment improved stress resistance, healthspan markers, and extended median lifespan by over 42%.
- **Organism/Model:** Human fibroblasts (in vitro); *C. elegans* (in vivo)

## 8. EPIGENETIC CLOCKS

**8.1** "DNA Methylation Age of Human Tissues and Cell Types"
- **Authors:** Horvath, S.
- **Journal:** Genome Biology, 14(10), R115
- **Year:** 2013
- **DOI:** [10.1186/gb-2013-14-10-r115](https://doi.org/10.1186/gb-2013-14-10-r115)
- **Summary:** Developed the first multi-tissue epigenetic clock using 353 CpG sites that accurately predicts chronological age across 51 healthy tissues and cell types (correlation 0.97, median error 2.9 years). The foundational biomarker for measuring biological age in reprogramming studies.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- 51 tissues and cell types (8,000 samples)

**8.2** "Genome-wide Methylation Profiles Reveal Quantitative Views of Human Aging Rates"
- **Authors:** Hannum, G., Guinney, J., Zhao, L. et al.
- **Journal:** Molecular Cell, 49(2), 359-367
- **Year:** 2013
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.molcel.2012.10.016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molcel.2012.10.016)
- **Summary:** Built a quantitative model of aging using 450,000+ CpG markers from whole blood of 656 individuals (ages 19-101), establishing the Hannum epigenetic clock with 71 key markers. Demonstrated that aging rates are impacted by gender and genetic variants.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- whole blood (656 individuals)

**8.3** "An Epigenetic Biomarker of Aging for Lifespan and Healthspan" (PhenoAge)
- **Authors:** Levine, M.E., Lu, A.T., Quach, A. et al.
- **Journal:** Aging (Albany NY), 10(4), 573-591
- **Year:** 2018
- **DOI:** [10.18632/aging.101414](https://doi.org/10.18632/aging.101414)
- **Summary:** Developed DNAm PhenoAge, a clock based on 513 CpGs optimized to predict phenotypic age (a multi-system proxy of physiological dysregulation) rather than chronological age. Strongly outperforms previous clocks in predicting all-cause mortality, cancer, healthspan, and Alzheimer's disease risk.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- blood and tissues

**8.4** "DNA Methylation GrimAge Strongly Predicts Lifespan and Healthspan"
- **Authors:** Lu, A.T., Quach, A., Wilson, J.G. et al. (Horvath lab)
- **Journal:** Aging (Albany NY), 11(2), 303-327
- **Year:** 2019
- **DOI:** [10.18632/aging.101684](https://doi.org/10.18632/aging.101684)
- **Summary:** Developed GrimAge, an epigenetic clock trained directly on time-to-death data using 8 DNA methylation-based surrogate markers for plasma proteins and smoking pack-years. Outperforms all previous clocks for predicting all-cause mortality, coronary heart disease, and cancer.
- **Organism/Model:** Human -- blood (Framingham Heart Study and other cohorts)

**8.5** "DNA Methylation-Based Biomarkers and the Epigenetic Clock Theory of Ageing"
- **Authors:** Horvath, S. & Raj, K.
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Genetics, 19(6), 371-384
- **Year:** 2018
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s41576-018-0004-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-018-0004-3)
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review unifying epigenetic clocks under a theory linking developmental and maintenance processes to biological aging. Proposes that epigenetic clocks capture a fundamental feature of the aging process across the entire life course.
- **Organism/Model:** Review -- human focus

## 9. SAFETY / CANCER RISK

**9.1** "Reprogramming In Vivo Produces Teratomas and iPS Cells with Totipotency Features"
- **Authors:** Abad, M., Mosteiro, L., Pantoja, C. et al. (Serrano lab, CNIO)
- **Journal:** Nature, 502(7471), 340-345
- **Year:** 2013
- **DOI:** [10.1038/nature12586](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature12586)
- **Summary:** Showed that continuous in vivo OSKM expression leads to teratoma formation across multiple organs, demonstrating that full reprogramming in vivo causes dangerous tumorigenic dedifferentiation. Critically established why partial/transient reprogramming is necessary rather than full reprogramming.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- whole organism (multiple organs)

**9.2** "Tissue Damage and Senescence Provide Critical Signals for Cellular Reprogramming In Vivo"
- **Authors:** Mosteiro, L., Pantoja, C., Alcazar, N. et al. (Serrano lab, CNIO)
- **Journal:** Science, 354(6315), aaf4445
- **Year:** 2016
- **DOI:** [10.1126/science.aaf4445](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aaf4445)
- **Summary:** Demonstrated that in vivo OSKM expression simultaneously induces senescence and reprogramming in nearby cells. Senescence, through the Ink4a/Arf locus and IL-6 secretion, creates a permissive tissue environment for reprogramming. Reveals the intimate connection between tumor suppression and reprogramming capacity.
- **Organism/Model:** Mouse -- multiple tissues

**9.3** "Prevention of Tumor Risk Associated with the Reprogramming of Human Pluripotent Stem Cells"
- **Authors:** Lee, A.S., Tang, C., Rao, M.S. et al.
- **Journal:** Journal of Experimental & Clinical Cancer Research, 39, 100
- **Year:** 2020
- **DOI:** [10.1186/s13046-020-01584-0](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13046-020-01584-0)
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review of strategies to prevent tumor risk in iPSC-derived cell therapies, including c-Myc exclusion, suicide gene systems, and selection methods. Addresses the critical safety challenge of translating reprogramming to clinical applications.
- **Organism/Model:** Review -- human iPSCs

## 10. NON-HUMAN PRIMATES

**10.1** "Senescence-Resistant Human Mesenchymal Progenitor Cells Counter Aging in Primates"
- **Authors:** Multiple authors
- **Journal:** Cell
- **Year:** 2025
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.cell.2025.05.021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2025.05.021)
- **Summary:** FOXO3-engineered senescence-resistant human mesenchymal progenitor cells (SRCs) were intravenously delivered to aged cynomolgus macaques over 44 weeks. Treatment improved cognitive performance, preserved brain structure, protected bone integrity, rejuvenated immune function, and reversed biological age across multiple tissues measured by DNA methylation clocks. No adverse effects detected.
- **Organism/Model:** Non-human primate -- cynomolgus macaque (*Macaca fascicularis*)

**10.2** Life Biosciences -- ER-100 NHP Studies (conference presentations, 2024-2025)
- **Authors:** Life Biosciences research team
- **Venue:** Presented at AAO 2024 and ARDD 2025
- **DOI:** Not yet available (conference data; peer-reviewed publication pending)
- **Summary:** ER-100, an AAV-delivered OSK gene therapy for partial epigenetic reprogramming, was administered via intravitreal injection to NHPs with induced optic neuropathy (NAION model). Significantly reduced deficits in pattern electroretinogram responses and axon density in both prevention and rescue models. Human clinical trials planned for 2026.
- **Organism/Model:** Non-human primate -- optic neuropathy model

## 11. REVIEWS

**11.1** "Partial Cellular Reprogramming: A Deep Dive into an Emerging Rejuvenation Technology"
- **Authors:** Paine, P.T., Nguyen, A. & Ocampo, A.
- **Journal:** Aging Cell, 23(2), e14039
- **Year:** 2024
- **DOI:** [10.1111/acel.14039](https://doi.org/10.1111/acel.14039)
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review exploring the aging-related benefits of partial cellular reprogramming, examining how reprogramming modulates cell fate and cellular age while harnessing rejuvenating benefits without altering cell identity. Covers in vivo and in vitro studies, safety concerns, and future directions.
- **Organism/Model:** Review

**11.2** "The Long and Winding Road of Reprogramming-Induced Rejuvenation"
- **Authors:** Ruetz, T.J., Pogson, A.N. & Bhatt, U. et al.
- **Journal:** Nature Communications, 15, 1941
- **Year:** 2024
- **DOI:** [10.1038/s41467-024-46020-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-46020-5)
- **Summary:** Critical review comparing partial reprogramming, full reprogramming, and transdifferentiation approaches for rejuvenation. Assesses the fundamental nature of aging through the lens of reprogramming and evaluates safety concerns for clinical translation.
- **Organism/Model:** Review

**11.3** "Cellular Reprogramming and Epigenetic Rejuvenation"
- **Authors:** Simpson, D.J., Olova, N.N. & Chandra, T.
- **Journal:** Clinical Epigenetics, 13, 170
- **Year:** 2021
- **DOI:** [10.1186/s13148-021-01158-7](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13148-021-01158-7)
